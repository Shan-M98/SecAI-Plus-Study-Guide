Domain 4: AI Governance, Risk, and Compliance

CompTIA SecAI+ CY0-001 — 19% of exam — Study Cheat Sheet

1. Organizational Controls: Governing AI Use

Concept What It Is Why It Matters
AI Center of Excellence A centralized cross-functional team that sets AI strategy, standards, policies, and best practices for the entire organization Provides unified governance, prevents fragmentation, ensures consistent risk management across all AI initiatives
Shadow AI Unsanctioned use of AI tools by employees without IT/security approval (e.g., pasting sensitive data into public ChatGPT) Data leakage, compliance violations, unvetted model outputs influencing decisions, no audit trail
Sanctioned AI AI tools approved, vetted, and monitored by the organization with defined acceptable use policies Controlled risk, data protection, audit logging, vendor assessment completed
Unsanctioned AI AI tools used without organizational approval — may or may not be malicious, but always ungoverned No security review, no data controls, no compliance alignment — the "Shadow AI" problem
Key Distinction: Shadow AI vs Unsanctioned AI

Shadow AI specifically refers to employees using AI tools without knowledge of IT/security (hidden use). Unsanctioned AI is the broader category: any AI not formally approved, whether hidden or simply not yet reviewed. All Shadow AI is unsanctioned, but not all unsanctioned AI is shadow (it might be known but not yet approved).

2. Responsible AI Principles

Principle Definition Example
Fairness AI systems produce equitable outcomes and do not discriminate against protected groups Auditing a hiring model to ensure it does not penalize applicants by gender or ethnicity
Transparency Stakeholders can see how the AI system operates — its data sources, training process, and decision pipeline are visible Publishing a model card that documents training data, architecture, and known limitations
Explainability A specific decision made by the AI can be understood and described in human terms "Your loan was denied because your debt-to-income ratio exceeded the threshold" — a clear reason for that decision
Accountability Clear ownership of AI outcomes — someone is responsible when the AI causes harm Designating a responsible AI officer who must sign off before a model goes to production
Key Distinction: Transparency vs Explainability (Exam Favorite!)
  • Transparency = Can you SEE the process? → "Here is our training data, architecture, and pipeline." (System-level visibility)
  • Explainability = Can you UNDERSTAND a specific decision? → "Here is why the model made this particular prediction." (Decision-level reasoning)

A model can be transparent (open-source, documented) but not explainable (deep neural net with no interpretable decision path). Conversely, a model can explain individual decisions (LIME/SHAP) while the overall system remains opaque.

3. Regulations & Frameworks

Framework Maintainer Scope Key Features
EU AI Act European Union Regulation (law) Risk-tiered classification:
Unacceptable Banned (social scoring, real-time biometric mass surveillance)
High-Risk Strict requirements (critical infrastructure, law enforcement, hiring)
Limited Transparency obligations (chatbots must disclose AI use)
Minimal No restrictions (spam filters, video games)
NIST AI RMF NIST (US) Voluntary framework 4 core functions:
Govern → culture, policies, roles for AI risk
Map → identify AI risks in context
Measure → assess and quantify risks
Manage → prioritize and treat risks
OECD AI Principles OECD (38 nations) Intergovernmental principles Promotes trustworthy AI: inclusive growth, human-centered values, transparency, robustness, accountability. Basis for many national AI policies.
ISO AI Standards ISO/IEC JTC 1/SC 42 International standards ISO/IEC 22989 — AI concepts and terminology
ISO/IEC 23894 — AI risk management guidance
ISO/IEC 42001 — AI management system (certifiable)
Key Distinction: EU AI Act vs NIST AI RMF

EU AI Act = legally binding regulation with enforcement and fines (like GDPR for AI). NIST AI RMF = voluntary, guidance-based framework (no penalties, but widely adopted as best practice). The EU Act classifies AI by risk tier; NIST RMF provides a process for managing AI risk regardless of tier.

4. Data Governance

Concept What It Means for AI
Data Sovereignty Data is subject to the laws and governance of the country/region where it is collected or stored. AI models trained on or processing data must comply with local regulations (e.g., EU data stays in EU for GDPR, cannot be sent to a US-hosted AI model without legal basis).
Data Classification Labels Tags applied to data indicating sensitivity level (Public, Internal, Confidential, Restricted). Determines which data can be used for AI training, which models can access it, and what controls are required. Prevents feeding classified data into public AI tools.

5. Deployment Models: Private vs Public AI

Factor Private AI Model Public AI Model
Data Exposure Data stays within org infrastructure — no third-party access Data sent to external vendor — potential leakage, training on your data
Customization Fine-tuned on proprietary data, tailored to org needs General-purpose, limited customization, may not fit domain
Compliance Full control over data residency, retention, and processing Must rely on vendor compliance certifications, DPAs
Cost High upfront (infrastructure, talent, training compute) Low upfront (pay-per-use), but ongoing API costs scale
Maintenance Org responsible for updates, patching, model drift monitoring Vendor handles updates, but you lose control over changes
Shadow AI Risk Lower — internal deployment is governed Higher — easy for employees to use unauthorized public tools
Key Distinction: Private vs Public AI Risk Profile

Private AI = more control, more responsibility. You own the risk of model quality, bias, and infrastructure security. Public AI = less control, shared responsibility. You trade data sovereignty for convenience. The exam focuses on when each is appropriate based on data sensitivity and compliance requirements.

6. Key Acronyms

ATLAS
Adversarial Threat Landscape for AI Systems
MITRE's knowledge base of adversarial tactics and techniques targeting ML systems (like ATT&CK but for AI)
RAG
Retrieval-Augmented Generation
Technique that grounds LLM responses in retrieved external data to reduce hallucinations and improve accuracy
GAN
Generative Adversarial Network
Two neural networks (generator + discriminator) competing to create realistic synthetic data; powers deepfakes
MLOps
Machine Learning Operations
Practices for deploying, monitoring, and maintaining ML models in production (DevOps for ML)
AI RMF
AI Risk Management Framework
NIST's voluntary framework with 4 functions (Govern, Map, Measure, Manage) for managing AI risks
MCP
Model Context Protocol
Open protocol standardizing how AI models connect to external tools and data sources
